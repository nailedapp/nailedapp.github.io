NailKeeper carved out a niche as one of the few apps built specifically for nail biters. It takes a straightforward approach: photograph your nails regularly and track their growth over time. For many people, that visual proof of progress is genuinely motivating.

But photo tracking has limits. If you're looking for something different—or something that works on a Mac—here's how the alternatives stack up.

## What NailKeeper Does Well

NailKeeper's core idea is solid. You snap photos of your nails on a schedule, and the app stores them so you can see how far you've come. It taps into a real psychological principle: visible progress reinforces behavior change.

The app works on iOS, it's simple to use, and it doesn't require any hardware beyond your iPhone. For people who bite their nails and want a visual record of recovery, it's purpose-built.

## Where NailKeeper Falls Short

Photo tracking is reactive, not proactive. You document what already happened—you don't get help in the moment when your fingers are heading toward your mouth. If you bite your nails unconsciously (which most habitual biters do), you might not even realize you've relapsed until you look at your nails hours later.

There's also the discipline problem. The app only works if you remember to take photos consistently. Skip a few days, and the tracking loses its power.

And there's no Mac version. If you spend most of your day working on a computer—which is when many people bite their nails—NailKeeper can't help you during those hours.

## NailKeeper Alternatives Worth Considering

### Nailed — Real-Time Detection on macOS

[Nailed](https://apps.apple.com/app/nailed-stop-biting-nails/id6761733224) takes a completely different approach. Instead of tracking damage after the fact, it uses your Mac's built-in camera and on-device machine learning to detect when your hand moves toward your mouth. When it spots the gesture, it flashes your screen and plays a beep—snapping you out of the habit in real time.

It sits in your menu bar, runs entirely on-device (no data leaves your Mac), and costs a one-time $4.99. No subscription.

**Where Nailed wins:** Real-time interruption of the habit. You don't need to remember to do anything—it watches for you. For people who bite unconsciously while working at their desk, this is a direct solution.

**Where Nailed doesn't fit:** It's macOS only. If you're not regularly at a Mac, it won't cover those hours. It also doesn't track nail growth—it's purely about catching and interrupting the behavior.

### Habit Tracking Apps (Habitica, Streaks, etc.)

General-purpose habit trackers let you log "didn't bite nails today" and build streaks. They're available on every platform and many are free. The downside is they rely entirely on your honesty and self-awareness. If you bite without noticing, you might mark a successful day that wasn't.

### Bitter Nail Polish

Not an app, but worth mentioning as an alternative. Products like Mavala Stop or Ella+Mila No More Biting put a foul taste on your nails. It's cheap, works on any platform (your hands), and doesn't require a device. But many habitual biters eventually get used to the taste. It also doesn't help you build awareness of the habit.

### Therapy-Based Approaches

Habit Reversal Training (HRT) and Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) have the strongest clinical evidence for stopping nail biting. You can pursue these through a therapist or through self-guided resources. Apps can support these approaches, but they're not substitutes for the underlying behavioral techniques.

### Wearable Devices (HabitAware Keen2)

The Keen2 bracelet detects hand-to-face movements using motion sensors. It vibrates when it catches you. This works regardless of platform and covers you all day, not just at a desk. The tradeoff is cost (~$150+) and the need to wear and charge another device.

## Which Approach Is Right for You?

It depends on your situation:

**Choose NailKeeper if** you've already started to reduce your biting and want to stay motivated by documenting nail regrowth. It's a good maintenance tool.

**Choose Nailed if** you're still actively biting—especially while working at your Mac—and need something to catch you in the moment. Real-time detection addresses the unconscious nature of the habit.

**Use both if** you want the best of both worlds. Nailed interrupts the habit at your desk; NailKeeper tracks the recovery on your phone.

**Choose a wearable if** you bite throughout the day in many contexts, not just at a computer. An all-day wearable covers more situations than any single app.

**Choose bitter polish if** you want a low-tech, low-cost solution and haven't tried it before. It works well for some people, especially as a first attempt.

## The Honest Take

There's no single perfect solution for nail biting. NailKeeper is a decent app that does one thing—track nail photos—and does it simply. If that's what you need, it works.

But if you need real-time intervention, NailKeeper doesn't provide it. That's not a flaw—it's a design choice. Different tools solve different parts of the problem.

The most effective approach for most people combines awareness (knowing when you bite), interruption (stopping yourself in the moment), and motivation (seeing progress over time). No single app covers all three. Pick the tools that address your biggest gap, and don't be afraid to combine them.
